# Unit — Competitors

Direct and adjacent competitors in the gym logging / workout tracking space. For each: positioning, strengths, weaknesses, and how Unit differs.

---

## 1. Hevy

- **One-line**: Social workout tracker with routines, rest timer, and progress charts.
- **Strengths**: Clean UI, routine templates, plate calculator, rest timer, Apple Watch, export.
- **Weaknesses**: Social feed and discovery can distract; setup can feel heavy for “program-first” users.
- **Unit difference**: No social or discovery. Focus on **your** program, **your** templates, and sub-3-second set logging (Gym Test). Structure over social.

---

## 2. Strong

- **One-line**: Simple strength tracker with exercises, workouts, and charts.
- **Strengths**: Straightforward, no fluff, one-time purchase option.
- **Weaknesses**: Less emphasis on “programs” and templates; some users want faster set entry.
- **Unit difference**: Program-first (DayTemplate → Session). Gym Test–first UX: defaults, minimal taps, optional RPE. SwiftData local-first, ready for sync later.

---

## 3. Fitbod

- **One-line**: AI-generated workout plans and recommendations.
- **Strengths**: Variety, recommendations, integration with wearables.
- **Weaknesses**: Opinionated plans and videos; not built for users who already have a program (e.g. from coach or spreadsheet).
- **Unit difference**: No AI plans or videos. You bring the program; Unit is the fast, structured logger that respects your template and your rest timer.

---

## 4. Apple Notes / Google Sheets

- **One-line**: Generic note-taking or spreadsheets used as workout logs.
- **Strengths**: Flexible, no app lock-in, works everywhere.
- **Weaknesses**: Unstructured, slow to log sets mid-workout, no rest timer, no templates or history UX.
- **Unit difference**: Dedicated schema (Exercise, DayTemplate, WorkoutSession, SetEntry), templates, one-tap set completion, and Live Activity rest timer. Structure and speed.

---

## 5. JEFIT / Gymaholic / Generic “bloated” trackers

- **One-line**: Full-featured fitness apps with exercise libraries, videos, social, and sometimes ads.
- **Strengths**: Large exercise databases, variety of features.
- **Weaknesses**: Clutter, videos, social feeds, and complex UIs slow down the core job: log sets under physical stress.
- **Unit difference**: Minimal surface. No feed, no videos, no discovery. One job: log your program fast (Gym Test) and see history. Best UX wins.

---

## 6. Strong App (StrongLifts 5x5) / Program-specific apps

- **One-line**: Apps tied to a specific program (e.g. 5x5).
- **Strengths**: Optimized for that program.
- **Weaknesses**: Not for users on custom or coach-given programs; limited to one methodology.
- **Unit difference**: Program-agnostic. You define DayTemplates and exercises; Unit supports any structure (Push/Pull/Legs, custom, etc.).

---

## 7. TrainHeroic / TeamBuildr (coach/team tools)

- **One-line**: Coach-assigned programs and team training.
- **Strengths**: Great for teams and assigned programs.
- **Weaknesses**: Overkill and often costly for individuals with their own program; not focused on “log in under 3 seconds.”
- **Unit difference**: Personal, local-first logger. You own your templates and data. No coach dashboard; just you and your program.

---

## Summary

| Competitor type | Unit position |
|-----------------|--------------------|
| Social / discovery trackers | No social; your program only. |
| Simple trackers | Add program/template-first and Gym Test UX. |
| AI / plan generators | No AI plans; you bring the program. |
| Notes/Sheets | Dedicated schema, templates, rest timer, fast entry. |
| Bloated trackers | Minimal surface; no videos, no feed. |
| Program-specific apps | Any program; you define templates. |

**Core differentiator**: Structured, fast-entry tool that passes the **Gym Test** (log a set in under 3 seconds) for users who already have their own program and are tired of messy notes or bloated apps.
